Problematising test

2017/07/13

Problematising process

The problematising process has moved the focus of my attention from from individual staff members to a closer lock at dominant culture within my organization.

Initially, I looked at vertical information flow from a very simplistic point of view. I asked, why does important information not reach the me? This question contains many problems in itself, such as a subjective definition of “important” (what’s important?). Through continued interaction with fellow students, literature and the field, I refined my problem statement to ’why does my company not have a proper, shared reporting structure’ and ’how can I establish a reporting structure that encourages people to pass on information?’.

How has your Critical Literature Review and other resources from the module supported your approach to solving your identified problem?

The critical literature review has provided the necessary background to probe reality. Through the review, I learned that employee voice is influenced by various factors from the domains of organizational culture and organizational design. For example, organizational culture include communication climate, and organizational design includes depth of hierarchy, and span of control, as highlighted by J. R. Detert and Burris (2007), J. Detert et al. (2013), Simpson (1959) and Mintzberg (1979). I also identified the areas of organizational identification and professional identification as viable origins of problems and thus further probing.

The discussion of paradigms and metaphors in Morgan (1980) has raised my awareness to identify common metaphors in conversations. Although Morgan referred to metaphors used in certain areas of research, I am able to identify metaphors also during conversations with peers and subjects. Just today, during an informal interview session with two of my delivery managers, it dawned on me that some of our problems are visible in the clients’ and in our’s language, and the different metaphors in use at two projects. Let’s briefly compare the two projects. In the first project, the client speaks of us as partners, all client managers talk about our staff as team members. In the second project, the client speaks of us as a vendor or supplier, and of our staff as resources. From our side, the first project contains highly committed staff, whereas the second project suffers from high attrition and generally low morale.

Of particular relevance were the concepts of knowledge creation and the different methodological tools associated with them. The research into paradigms and worldviews (Creswell 2014) yielded the right methodological tools to understand the problem in depth. By gaining this increased understanding, I am able to provide more relevant solutions.

Desired outcomes

Organisationally

Initially, I approached my problem under the assumption that some individuals need different instructions or a different modality of contact with me. I falsely located the problem solely in individual staff to not forward information along vertical communication structures. The works of

Through conversations in our classroom, and Edward in particular, my initial rough sketch of a the problem and its solution changed to fix a possible cultural issue within my units. Ideally, I achieve to change three areas: (1) corporate culture to change the identification routines, (2) also, I want to build a culture of trust and (3) a climate of safety to speak up.

Personally

This module has familiarized me with the most relevant philosophical worldviews on knowledge creation, science, and research. The classroom discussions about four paradigms of Morgan (1980), regardless of whether they are incommensurable or not, have started a period of search for myself - what worldviews do I share, and what are my core values as a researcher. Our classroom discussions have pushed me forward on this journey: the need to reflect on others’ postings triggered extensive periods of literature research and critical reflection about myself, my position and what I want to achieve. In summary, I was able to locate myself in the phenomenological and ethnographic space, as a social constructivist. Over this and subsequent modules, I will try to hone these areas.

Influence of this module’s ideas and practices

Let’s put it straight right here, the amount of philosophical concepts was overwhelming. Positive epistemology, positivism, antipositivism, constructionism, etc. - the words started to make sense after some time. The different philosophical stances presented in this module have made me more aware of how some people think and work. So far, this module has added tremendously to my knowledge about how to understand and resolve the problem. Predominantly, these two benefits result from readings like Morgan (1980), Huff (2000), Rynes, Bartunek, and Daft (2001) and Huff (2009). Critical literature, such as Lex Donaldson (1998; L. Donaldson 2008) have added another perspective on Morgan’s work. These interactions with literature, and continued research into the topic have exposed myself to phenomenology, ethnography, radical humanist views and social constructivism and many other ideas. Exposure to postmodernism made me anchor myself even more in the social constructivism space. Last but not least, Morgan’s ideas about paradigms and metaphors have raised my awareness of my and my peers’, and literature’s language and the underlying assumptions conveyed. As a concrete example, inline with literature, I concur calling the act of passing information to one’s manager ’vertical communication’. This metaphor conveys the image of a pyramid, with some people being on top and others at the bottom. As a result, I intend to isolate my concepts from my the perception of my subjects and I try to not use my metaphor during qualitative interviews to reduce my influence on the outcome of my research.

Moving forward in the problem-solving process

Based on the above, my next steps include an informed data acquisition through phenomenology inspired interviews. How this is going to look like is subject of ongoing research into best practices. Once the results are collected, I am going to reflect over these interviews to see if the recorded narratives match theory. In the best case, staff expresses similar reasons, either consciously or subconsciously through metaphors, etc. as identified during my literature research. Equipped with these results, I will kick off some improvement initiatives, especially in the area of staff’s identification.
